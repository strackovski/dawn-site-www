THE DAWN SPACEPROBE

First spacecraft to orbit a dwarf planet

About Dawn

DAWN is a space probe launched by NASA in 2007 to study the two most massive protoplanets of the asteroid belt: Vesta and the dwarf planet Ceres. Dawn has orbited one member of the main asteroid belt, Vesta, on July 16, 2011 and is now heading to explore a second new world, dwarf planet Ceres, whose orbit it entered on March 6, 2015.

With its solar array in the retracted launch position, the Dawn spacecraft is 2.36 meters (7.7 ft) long. With its solar arrays fully extended, Dawn is 19.7 m (65 ft) long. Total area of solar arrays is 36.4 m2 (392 sq ft).

The Dawn spacecraft is propelled by three xenon ion thrusters that have a specific impulse of 3,100 s and produce a thrust of 90 mN.

With the propellant it carries, Dawn can perform a velocity change of more than 10 km/s over the course of its mission, far more than any previous spacecraft achieved with on-board propellant after separation from its launch rocket.

Dawn carries a memory chip bearing the names of more than 360,000 space enthusiasts. The names were submitted online as part of a public outreach effort between September 2005 and November 2006.

Dwarf planet

The International Astronomical Union (IAU) adopted a new definition of planet on August 24, 2006, which introduced the term "dwarf planet" for ellipsoidal worlds that were too small to qualify for planetary status by "clearing their orbital neighborhood" of other orbiting matter. A dwarf planet is therefore a planetary-mass object that is in direct orbit of the Sun, and is massive enough for its shape to be in hydrostatic equilibrium under its own gravity (nearly round), but has not cleared the neighborhood around its orbit.

About Ceres

Ceres is the largest object in the asteroid belt, the region of the Solar System between the orbits of the planets Mars and Jupiter, occupied by numerous asteroids and minor planets. It is composed of rock and ice, is 950 km in diameter, and comprises about one-third of the total mass of the bodies in the asteroid belt, which is in turn about 4% of the mass of the Moon.

The Mission

DAWN's goal is to characterize the conditions and processes of its earliest history by investigating in detail two of the largest protoplanets remaining intact since their formation. Ceres and Vesta reside in the main asteroid belt, the extensive region between Mars and Jupiter, along with many other smaller bodies. Each followed a very different evolutionary path, constrained by the diversity of processes that operated during the first few million years of solar system evolution.

The top level question that the mission addresses is the role of size and water in determining the evolution of the planets. Ceres and Vesta are the right two bodies with which to address this question, as they are the most massive of the protoplanets, baby planets whose growth was interrupted by the formation of Jupiter. Ceres is very primitive and wet while Vesta is evolved and dry.

Available evidence indicates that both bodies formed very early in the history of the Solar System, thereby retaining a record of events and processes from the time of the formation of the terrestrial planets. Radionuclide dating of pieces of meteorites thought to come from Vesta suggests that Vesta differentiated quickly, in three million years or less. Thermal evolution studies suggest that Ceres must have formed some time later, more than three million years after the formation of the oldest known objects of Solar System origin.

Objectives

  • Provide data on the role of size and water in planetary evolution
  • Help in understanding the origin and evolution of the solar system
  • Get an insight into the conditions under which Vesta and Ceres formed
  • Determine the nature of the building blocks from which the terrestrial planets formed to improve the scientific understanding of this formation
  • Determine what factors controlled the different evolution of the two protoplanets

Achievements

  • First NASA exploratory mission to use ion propulsion to enter orbits (creates thrust by accelerating ions)
  • First spacecraft to visit Vesta
  • First spacecraft to visit Ceres
  • First spacecraft to orbit a dwarf planet
  • First spacecraft to orbit two separate extraterrestrial bodies
